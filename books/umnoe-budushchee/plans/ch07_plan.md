# Chapter Plan

## Goal

This chapter must introduce the younger version of the old man as the finder of the manuscript and shift the story from warning to reception. The discovery should not feel miraculous; it should feel accidental at first, then gradually morally inevitable.

## Key Events

- Time has passed since the traveler wrote the book.
- The younger old man encounters the manuscript in a believable, grounded circumstance.
- He initially treats it as strange, possibly fictional or delusional material.
- Something in the text or in current reality creates the first serious crack in his doubt.
- He continues reading instead of dismissing it.
- The chapter ends with the first feeling that the book may be telling the truth.

## Character Focus

- The younger finder becomes the new active center of the plot.
- He should feel distinct from the traveler: less broken, more open, but not naive.
- The old man in the frame remains present as a memory-bearer who is quietly circling his younger self.

## Emotional Beats

- Curiosity
- Mild skepticism
- Uneasy recognition
- Moral pull
- Quiet commitment

## Setup For Future

- Prepare chapter 8 by planting the finder's first deep intellectual breakthrough.
- Start linking details from the manuscript to the finder's real world.
- Make the book feel like an object that carries both fragility and strange authority.

## Constraints

- Do not make the discovery feel like destiny in a mystical sense.
- Keep the finder's reaction psychologically credible; he should not believe too quickly.
- Preserve the denser paragraphing style and the intimate frame voice.
